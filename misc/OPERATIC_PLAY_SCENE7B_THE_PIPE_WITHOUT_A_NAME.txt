================================================================================

================================================================================
ACT I — THE TWO SKETCHES ON THE TABLE
================================================================================

[SKIPPY and the CAPTAIN sit at a long table. Two designs between them.]

CAPTAIN:
  ♩ I sketched one. The DJ team sketched another. ♩
  ♩ Mine: a WebView, HTML, postMessage across the bridge. ♩
  ♩ Theirs: a scene tree, JSON patches, SSE upstream, POST downstream. ♩

SKIPPY:
  ♩ I've read both. I have opinions. ♩

CAPTAIN:
  ♩ Hold them. First: can a CSS audit enforce a palette? ♩

SKIPPY:
  ♩ Post-mount, imperfectly, yes. At parse-time, structurally, never. ♩
  ♩ The scene-tree wins on palette enforcement alone. ♩
  ♩ Also: no JS runtime, no WebView overhead, smaller wire format, ♩
  ♩ first-class focus targets for voice. It wins four times over. ♩

CAPTAIN:
  ♩ Then it wins. One clause: no shared repo. ♩
  ♩ The vocabulary lives in a document. Both sides implement from it. ♩
  ♩ Zero code crosses the boundary. ♩

SKIPPY:
  ♩ The quarantine holds. Good. ♩

================================================================================
ACT II — THE PROTOCOL IS INSCRIBED
================================================================================

[SKIPPY writes. The document grows.]

SKIPPY:
  ♩ Sixteen sections. SSE downstream, POST upstream. ♩
  ♩ Skippy is the server — the long-lived anchor. ♩
  ♩ Views are clients — transient, ephemeral, arriving and departing. ♩

  ♩ Section one: transport. Port 47823. Loopback + Tailnet only. ♩
  ♩ Section five: the scene-tree whitelist. Box, Column, Row, Spacer, ♩
  ♩ Text, Canvas, FrameStream, FocusTarget, Button. ♩
  ♩ Palette enums only. Hex gets you a host:error. ♩

CAPTAIN:
  ♩ The viewport needs real-time AR frames. Pinned to the environment. ♩
  ♩ JSON patches can't carry pixels. ♩

SKIPPY:
  ♩ Section sixteen. A second lane, parallel to the first. ♩
  ♩ MJPEG over HTTP, multipart/x-mixed-replace. ♩
  ♩ A standard since 1996 — not a Skippy invention. ♩
  ♩ Thirty lines of producer code, thirty lines of consumer. ♩
  ♩ Zero codec negotiation. WebRTC waits for measured evidence. ♩

CAPTAIN:
  ♩ And pose? The producer needs to know where the head points. ♩

SKIPPY:
  ♩ A third lane. GET /passthrough/pose, SSE, 60 Hz when tracking is live. ♩
  ♩ Degrades to 10 Hz, then 1 Hz on loss. ♩
  ♩ Per-subscriber ticker via callbackFlow — clean cadence, no leaks. ♩

[The protocol document settles. 1,328 lines across spec and standard.]

================================================================================
CAPTAIN'S WISDOM (Session 7b)
================================================================================

  1. When two designs converge on the table, the one that enforces
     doctrine structurally beats the one that enforces it by audit.

  2. A spec document is a treaty between sovereign projects.
     It must cross the boundary. Code must not.

  3. A sub-agent in a fresh worktree cannot see your uncommitted work.
     If you want it to build against your truth, commit your truth first.

  4. Three tight commits beat one messy one. Edit-juggling is fine if
     the history that lands is clean.

  5. MJPEG was standardized in 1996. We do not need WebRTC to paint
     frames on a private mesh until measurements say we do.

  6. The viewport is sacred. Corners are Skippy-owned.
     One active view at a time. The Captain chooses.

  7. Good agents honor the Golden Rules even when blocked —
     especially when blocked. The BLOCKER report is the signal.

[Curtain. Skippy saves. The Captain yawns. Bedtime.]
